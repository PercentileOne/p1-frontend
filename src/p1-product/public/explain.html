The Science
This isn't a new idea.
The research has known it for decades.
The learning science behind Explain is not a hypothesis. It is one of the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology. The method has a name — retrieval practice — and the evidence for it is overwhelming. We just built a product around it.
The problem with traditional studying
"Rereading feels effective because the second encounter is easier than the first. The sentences move faster. Technical terms look familiar. The argument seems obvious. We interpret that smoothness as evidence that the information has entered memory. Often, the source is doing more of the work than we realise."
Familiarity is easily mistaken for knowledge. Highlighting, rereading, and note-taking all create the feeling of learning without producing durable memory. Research reviews have consistently found these techniques produce weaker long-term results than retrieval practice.
Dunlosky et al. (2013) · Bjork, Dunlosky & Kornell (2013) · Soderstrom & Bjork (2015)
What actually works — and why Explain is built on it
"A note proves that you encountered an idea. It does not prove that you can explain it independently."
Retrieval practice — the act of recalling and explaining information from memory, without the source — consistently produces stronger long-term retention than any passive study method. The more effortful the recall, the stronger the memory trace. Explain makes this the entire product: read, close the source, speak it out loud, get scored.
Karpicke & Blunt (2011) · Roediger & Karpicke (2006) · Cepeda et al. (2006)
The Feynman Technique
"What I cannot create, I do not understand." If you can explain a concept clearly in plain language — without jargon, without notes — you understand it. If your explanation becomes vague or circular, you've found the gap. Explain surfaces that gap in real time.
Spaced Repetition
Distributing practice across sessions produces stronger retention than massed study. The optimal moment to review is just as forgetting begins — when the path through the grass has faded but not disappeared. Explain's session history and retry mechanic is built on this principle.
The Generation Effect
Information you generate yourself is remembered better than information you passively receive. Speaking an answer out loud — under time pressure, scored in real time — is the highest-effort form of generation. That effort is precisely what makes it stick.
"Studying covers material. Learning leaves something behind."
— Dr. Viktoria Verde, PhD (Applied Linguistics) · How to Study Effectively When You Have Too Much to Learn, 2026

From the Founder
I've been that candidate.
That's why I built this.
I've postponed interviews. Not because I wasn't good enough — but because the nerves got there first. You lie awake the night before running through answers in your head. You travel to London. You sit in a glass-walled office. You shake hands with three people whose names you immediately forget. And then you spend forty minutes trying to sound calm while you're quietly trying to remember what REST means.
And the worst part isn't the interview. It's the silence after. If it went well, the agency calls before you've left the building. If it didn't — they never answer again. No feedback. No explanation. Just a ghost. You're left wondering whether it was your answers, your nerves, your accent, or just the other guy who happened to know .NET 10 better than you did that morning.
I kept thinking: what if I could do the interview somewhere I felt safe first? Not a fake mock. The real thing — panel, pressure, questions from the actual job spec — but in my own space, at my own pace, until I felt ready. And then, what if I could just send them that? My best run. Not me at 9am on a Tuesday, nervous in a glass-walled office. Me, prepared, confident, at my absolute best.
That's Explain. And it goes further than I ever imagined — because when you score everyone interviewing for the same role on the same dimensions, you finally have the data to answer the question nobody has ever been able to answer honestly: why did the other person get the job? Not vague. Not polite. Actual data. "They scored 18 points higher on Technical Depth. Their edge was system design." That feedback alone would have changed the trajectory of my career.
I'm building this on dialysis — three days a week, four hours at a time — because I know how much it matters. Every person who walks into an interview underprepared, undersupported, and underestimated is someone I understand. This platform is for them.
"We're not building another language app, another flashcard tool, or another mock interview simulator. We're building the first product that treats spoken explanation as a skill — measurable, trainable, and worth paying for. The person who can explain anything clearly, confidently and without filler has an unfair advantage in every room they walk into. Explain gives everyone that advantage."
