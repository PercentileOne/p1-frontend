The Panel Simulator

Each panellist has a personality.
And they're watching.

One evaluates everything you say. One goes three levels deep on every answer. One challenges you regardless of how well you're doing. Their reactions shift in real time — based on how you're performing.

🔵
The Evaluator
Scores every answer methodically. Impressed by structure and clarity. Neutral until you earn it. Becomes visibly warmer as your scores improve.
🟣
The Deep Diver
Follows up every answer with a harder question. Doesn't accept surface-level answers. "Can you go deeper on that?" is a good sign, not a bad one.
🔴
The Skeptic
Challenges everything. Not impressed easily. Every real interview has one. Explain puts them in every panel so you stop being surprised by them.
Filler Word Detection
Nobody forgets their score the first time they see "basically × 18."
Live detection of "um", "uh", "like", "basically", "you know". After every session, Explain shows your filler word count, tracks whether it's improving across sessions, and flags the moments they appeared in your transcript. Interviewers notice. Now you will too.
Surprise MCQ
Mid-interview. Countdown timer. No warning.
At a random point in your session, the panel pauses and drops a multiple-choice question from the job spec — timed. Real interviews test how you think under pressure, not just when you're comfortable. The Surprise MCQ makes sure you're ready for that too.

About

Built by someone who has been in that chair.

Over 25 years of commercial software development. Dozens of interviews — on both sides of the table. Technical panels, competency rounds, visa appointments, the lot. I know exactly what it feels like to walk in underprepared. And I know what it feels like to walk out knowing you nailed it.

Explain exists because the gap between those two experiences is entirely fixable — with the right preparation. Not generic tips. Not a list of "common interview questions." A real simulation, built from your actual job spec, that forces you to speak your answers out loud until they're good.

The Feynman Technique says if you can't explain something simply, you don't understand it well enough. Explain turns that principle into a product — for interviews, for exams, for every high-stakes moment where words matter.
